Local-first assistive technology describes tools that process user data on the user's device or inside a controlled local runtime. For elderly and low-vision users, local tools can help summarize long articles, read documents, convert forms into tables, and describe images without sending private files to third-party inference APIs.

The main design tradeoff is that small local models may be slower or less capable than large hosted systems. A routed small-model stack can reduce this problem by assigning text, vision, speech, and export work to specialized local components.

For accessibility, useful systems should use simple language, large controls, visible warnings, and clear uncertainty. Financial, medical, and legal outputs should be treated as drafts that require human review.
